Blocks of Change

How a simple soil-press is building dignified spaces — and preparing a people to stand.

"A people who have never learned to work with their hands, to build shelter, to depend on what the earth provides — cannot stand in the hour of final crisis."

That conviction sits at the heart of everything Solvēnce is building. Not as an idea. As a practice. And sometimes the clearest way to explain a mission is to point at a small building on a hillside in upcountry Kenya and say: it started here.

The Old Latrine

Every home tells a story, including the parts we try not to look at. The old outhouse on my family's upcountry plot told one of quiet neglect — not from laziness, but from the all-too-familiar arithmetic of poverty and distance. It was a structure cobbled together from rusted iron sheets, its roof sagging, its frame held up by poles that had long since begun to rot through.

The old structure. Rusted sheets, rotten pole slab. Functional, barely. Dignified, not at all.

It did a job. But it did nothing for the dignity of the people who used it. And dignity is not a luxury — it is the starting point. You cannot ask a person to stand tall in the things that matter most if the most private corners of their daily life speak a different word about their worth.

What Are ISSBs?

Interlocking Stabilised Soil Blocks — ISSBs — are compressed earth blocks made primarily from on-site soil, stabilised with a small proportion of cement (typically 5–8%) and pressed into precision-shaped bricks using a manual or mechanical block press. The interlocking geometry means the blocks fit together without the thick mortar joints conventional brickwork demands. No kiln. No firing. Minimal cement. Maximum use of what the land already gives you.

Blocks pressed on site. The raw material was already underfoot.

The result is a block that is stronger than fired brick in compression, breathes naturally, regulates temperature, and — critically — costs a fraction of conventional masonry.

~50% Reduction in material costs vs. fired brick
5–8% Cement by volume — the rest is local soil
0 Kilns. No burning. Low embodied energy.

Half the construction cost is not a minor detail. In communities where building a simple home can consume a decade of savings, cutting that figure in half is the difference between a dream deferred indefinitely and a home your children can grow up in now.

Building the New Outhouse

The project was modest by any measure. A replacement latrine — the kind of build that rarely makes portfolios. But that modesty is precisely why it belongs here. Solvēnce is not about grand gestures for paying clients only. It is about proving the method in real life, at real scale, with real limitations.

Walls rising. Each course interlocks with the one below — no deep mortar beds required.

The soil was excavated from the site itself, screened, and pressed into blocks. The foundation was set, the courses went up clean and level — each block clicking into the geometry of its neighbour — and the structure went from bare earth to roofable walls in a matter of days, not weeks.

Walls complete. The precision of the interlock means no thick mortar joints — less material, more structure.

Where the old structure was rust and rot, the new one stands in solid masonry. Where the old one apologised for itself, the new one holds its head up. It is a latrine. It is also a demonstration. And it is also a statement about what people in rural Kenya deserve to wake up to every morning.

Done. A modern, solid, dignified outhouse — built from the ground it stands on. Inside. Clean lines, solid walls, natural ventilation. No apology necessary.

Why This Matters to the Mission

The mark of the beast is not a distant abstraction — it is the imminent test of every soul's loyalty. A people who have never learned to depend on God, to work with their hands, to grow their food, or to think freely cannot stand in that hour.

The Solvēnce mission is to prepare a people to stand. That word — prepare — implies time, practice, and habit. You do not become a person who can stand in the final crisis by reading about it. You become that person by spending your years building something real, cultivating something living, and learning to provide from what God has already placed within reach.

ISSBs fit into this vision at a structural level. They represent a technology that:

  • Returns power to the builder. The material is underfoot. The skill is learnable. The dependency on distant supply chains is cut.
  • Makes housing accessible. Cutting construction costs in half means communities that have been excluded from solid shelter can now build it.
  • Demonstrates right relationship with creation. Using what the earth provides — responsibly, skillfully, without waste — is not merely economic. It is a theology of stewardship.
  • Is replicable. Every family that sees this latrine is seeing a proof of concept for their own home, their own school, their own church.

The Solvēnce Home is not a charity. It is an institution being built — slowly, carefully, from the ground up — that will one day train communities in the skills of self-sufficient life: growing food, building shelter, understanding health, thinking clearly, and walking with God. This latrine is step one of one thousand steps. And step one is always the most important.

You cannot build a people out of thin air. You build them block by block, habit by habit, day by day. The earth gives the material. The hands do the work. And God gives the increase.
· · ·

What Comes Next

This project is a first proof. The ambition is larger: full residential structures, community facilities, demonstration workshops where families can learn to press and lay their own blocks. If you can build a dignified outhouse from your own soil, you can build a home. If you can build a home, you can build a community. If you can build a community, you have the foundation for an institution.

The Solvēnce Home is under construction — in every sense of the phrase. More builds, more stories, more proof will follow. Stay with it.
